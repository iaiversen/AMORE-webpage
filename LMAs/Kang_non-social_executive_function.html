Abstract

This preregistered systematic review and meta-analysis investigates the effects of oxytocin administration on non-social executive functions in humans. While oxytocin has received considerable attention for its role in social behaviors, emerging evidence suggests broader cognitive effects beyond social contexts.

The analysis employed both frequentist and Bayesian approaches, synthesizing 20 effect estimates from 13 eligible studies (729 total participants). The project addresses whether oxytocin administration influences executive functions when measured using non-social stimuli and tasks. A multilevel random-effects meta-analysis was conducted to account for multiple outcomes within studies.

Current Results

The meta-analysis found no overall significant effect of oxytocin administration on non-social executive functions (Hedges' g = 0.07, 95% CI [-0.07, 0.22], p = 0.30).

However, subgroup analyses revealed significant heterogeneity across different executive function domains, suggesting that oxytocin's effects may be domain-specific rather than uniformly distributed across all cognitive functions.

Bayesian Meta-Analysis Results
The Robust Bayesian Meta-Analysis provided moderate support for the absence of an overall effect (BF₁₀ = 0.10) and moderate evidence against publication bias (BF_PB = 0.32). These findings strengthen confidence in the null result for the overall analysis.
Statistical Power Analysis
Individual studies were generally underpowered (5-10% median power) to detect the observed summary effect size. This highlights the importance of meta-analytic approaches for detecting small but meaningful effects in oxytocin research.
Figure 2: Firepower plot. Statistical power of the main meta-analysis obtained by calculating the median power for effect sizes ranging from Hedges' g = 0.1–1 across all studies included in the meta-analysis. The analysis shows that individual studies had very low statistical power (median 6%) to detect the observed summary effect size.

Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Inclusion Criteria

  • Type of Studies: Human studies measuring effects of oxytocin administration on executive function using non-social stimuli
  • Intervention: Oxytocin administered intranasally, orally, or intravenously regardless of dosage
  • Population: Both clinical and neurotypical participants (no age restrictions)
  • Outcomes: Executive function measures (working memory, inhibitory control, cognitive flexibility) using non-social stimuli such as abstract shapes
  • Design: Placebo-controlled studies
  • Language: Publications in English language
  • Data Requirements: Sufficient statistical information to calculate effect sizes

Exclusion Criteria

  • Non-human animal studies
  • Studies investigating only endogenous oxytocin levels (no administration)
  • Studies measuring only somatic functions
  • Executive function tasks using social stimuli (e.g., emotional faces, social scenarios)
  • Studies without placebo control conditions
  • Insufficient statistical data for meta-analysis

Methodology Summary

This preregistered systematic review followed PRISMA guidelines. Searches were conducted in PubMed, Europe PubMed Central, and Bielefeld Academic Search Engine (BASE) on July 29, 2022, with an updated search on August 1, 2023.

Quality assessment was conducted using the RoB 2.0 tool for assessing risk of bias in randomized trials. Two reviewers independently extracted data and assessed study quality, with disagreements resolved through discussion.

Statistical analyses were implemented in R Studio 4.2.2 using the metafor package for frequentist meta-analysis and RoBMA for Robust Bayesian Meta-Analysis. A multilevel random-effects model was used to account for multiple dependent outcomes from the same studies. Between-measurement correlation was set at r = 0.3 based on available raw data from one included study.

Study Characteristics

  • Included studies: The final meta-analysis included 13 studies with 20 effect estimates
  • Total participants: 729 (284 in within-participant studies, 445 in between-group studies)
  • Mean age: 31.64 years (within-participant studies), 25.68 years (between-group studies)
  • Oxytocin dosage: Predominantly 24 IU intranasal (76.92% of studies)
  • Administration routes: Intranasal (92.3%), oral (7.7%)
  • Publication status: 76.92% peer-reviewed articles, 23.07% letters to editor
  • Study designs: 6 within-participant, 7 between-group studies
